On the Nature of Research

Reflections on curiosity, persistence, and the pursuit of understanding

Research is often portrayed as a linear journey from question to answer. The reality, I've found, is far more circuitous and infinitely more interesting.

The Myth of the Eureka Moment

Popular culture loves the story of sudden insight—Newton's apple, Archimedes' bathtub. But real research rarely works this way. Progress comes through countless small steps, failed experiments, and ideas that seem promising but lead nowhere.

This isn't discouraging; it's liberating. Understanding that struggle is part of the process removes the pressure to have brilliant insights on demand.

The Value of Being Stuck

Some of my most productive periods have come after being thoroughly stuck on a problem. The frustration of not understanding something deeply drives a kind of engagement that casual interest never achieves.

When stuck, I try to articulate exactly what I don't understand. Often, the act of precisely stating my confusion reveals the path forward.

Collaboration and Solitude

Research requires both. Conversations with others expose blind spots in my thinking and introduce perspectives I'd never find alone. But deep work requires extended periods of focused solitude.

Finding the right balance is an ongoing challenge. Too much isolation leads to tunnel vision; too much collaboration fragments attention.

Writing as Thinking

I've come to believe that writing isn't just how we communicate research—it's how we do research. The act of explaining something precisely often reveals gaps in understanding that felt solid in my head.

Looking Forward

As I continue on this path, I try to maintain the curiosity that drew me to research in the first place. The questions that excite me may change, but the fundamental drive to understand remains constant.
